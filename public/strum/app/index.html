Meet your guitar

Before you can play it, it helps to know what everything is called. Tap any part below to learn about it.

Tap any part of the guitar above.

02

The six strings

A guitar has six strings, each one with its own name. The thickest is on top, the thinnest is on the bottom. Tap each one to hear it!

A way to remember the names from thickest to thinnest: Eat A Donut, Get Better Eating. Each first letter is a string — E, A, D, G, B, E.

03

Music notes

All of music — every song you've ever heard — is made from just seven letter names: A, B, C, D, E, F, G. After G, it loops right back to A. Tap each one to hear it.

Look familiar? Some of the string names you just learned (E, A, D, G, B) are music notes too! That's because each string is tuned to play a specific note when you pluck it open.

04

Higher and lower

Some notes sound like a bird up in the sky. Others sound like a big truck rumbling. We call this pitch — how high or low a note sounds. Listen!

Thicker, longer strings sound LOWER. Thinner, shorter strings sound HIGHER. That's true on every instrument — even your own voice.

05

What's a fret?

Frets are the little metal bars on the long part of the guitar. When you press a string against one and pluck, the note goes higher. The closer to your body, the higher the sound.

Hear how each one is a little higher than the last? That's because the string gets shorter when you press behind a higher fret.

06

Open and pressed

"Open" means you don't put any finger on the string — just pluck it. "Pressed" means you hold down a fret first. Both ways make notes, just different ones.

Open string
No fingers. Just pluck.
Pressed at fret 3
Finger holds down the string.

Notice the pressed note sounds higher than the open one. Pressing makes the string shorter, and shorter strings make higher notes.

07

The beat

All music has a steady beat — like a heartbeat. It just keeps going: 1, 2, 3, 4… 1, 2, 3, 4. Press start and clap along!

1
2
3
4

Most songs count to 4 and start over. When you play guitar, your strums sit right on those beats. That's why a metronome (in Practice) helps so much — it keeps the beat steady for you.

08

What's a chord?

A chord is just several notes played at the same time. When you strum across the strings while pressing the right ones, all those notes come out together.

Strum all 6 strings with this hand shape and you've played an Em chord. That's three different notes (E, G, and B) all at once.

09

Strumming — down and up

Strumming is brushing the pick across the strings. You can go down (from the thick string toward the thin one) or up (the other way). Songs usually mix them.

10

Reading TAB

TAB is a special way to write guitar music. It looks tricky but it's actually really simple — six lines and some numbers!

11

13

Why the same four chords are in every song

Every key has a family of seven chords. Pop music basically lives on four of them: I, IV, V, vi. That's why your seven chords cover most songs.

The capo is a transpose button

Your piano teacher had you transpose pieces — play the same melody starting on a different key. A capo does exactly that on guitar. Clamp at fret N and every shape sounds N half-steps higher.
